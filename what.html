Going to the Internship Fair

I'd never thought I'd work out the courage to go to the internship fair, but then, before I knew it, my second year at Bradley slammed into me like a car against a brick wall. Most of my friends had already got internships and work in their fields of study. Meanwhile, I could barely do chores without messing up royally, let alone holding down an actual job. I realized that if I wanted to do something with my life, I'd have to change.
I remember walking into the foyer of the Renaissance Colosseum that fateful day. Everywhere I saw were people in business suits, carrying shining briefcases, their faces glowing with the confidence of corporate executives. I felt sick in the stomach, unsure if the feeling was courage, nervousness, or dread. It was like staring into the face of a titan who was about to grab me up and eat me. I was surrounded by people who were better than me in every way, and yet, they were my competition, my enemies.
When I entered the main fairground, a thousand thoughts poured into my head like rays of light: my profile, my projects, the employers and what their companies did. I wanted to just close my eyes, to tune them out, to go into my happy place. When I walked up to the first employer, I could barely say my class year and degree. My mind went blank, my INFOmercial collapsed before it even got off the ground. Meanwhile, I was bombarded with questions: "What languages do you know? Do you have any work experience? Describe to me a project you did in Java." My thoughts flowed through my mind like a waterfall, and I just couldn't keep up with them.
I walked away wanted to throw up. I just wanted to run out that door and never come back again. Yet I heard my voice scream in the back of my head: "You're a sophomore and you can't even design a webpage! If you can't get a little internship, Microsoft and Google would never hire you. You're a failure as a computer scientist!" This was going to be a long day.
